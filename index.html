New FIA Regulations to Combat 'Porpoising' for the 2023 Formula One Season

Writer: Shree Murthy


Porpoising: What is it?

Porpoising is an aerodynamic phenomenon caused by the 2022 F1 car design, which intended to create more downforce and has created a side effect known as porpoising. It has been seen affecting many drivers and cars, causing discomfort for drivers. Porpoising can actually be tuned out of the car, however this will result in loss in performance.

Fernando Alonso experiences porpoising at the British Grand Prix 2022


How did porpoising begin? Well, in the aftermath of a car, there is turbulent, "dirty" air. This contaminated air makes it challenging for a motorist in pursuit to come close, maintain speed, and pass the leading vehicle. The 2022 car design regulations implemented several structural modifications to encourage closer racing and more precise passing.

Some structural changes included a wider front wing, over-wheel winglets and tunnels underneath the chassis floor. This brought the unexpected side effect of porpoising. Porpoising occurs when a car's downforce draws it so close to the ground at high speeds that it obstructs airflow and causes an abrupt loss of downforce. As this cycle repeats, the suspension pulls the car back up, causing it to bounce.

The (FIA) Solution?

After several driver complaints regarding porpoising, the FIA has decided to take action. The organisation first implemented a technical directive, specifically directive TD039, which allows the FIA to monitor vertical accelerations and place limits on the levels of these accelerations (technical directives are a form of rules that are typically discussed mainly among teams). From the Belgian Grand Prix, the FIA will keep vertical accelerations of cars underneath a microscope and take action against any vehicles considered to be 'extreme'.
